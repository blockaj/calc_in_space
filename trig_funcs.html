Trigonometric Functions

Timmy is in a jet pack. He has an equation to calculate the atmospheric pressure up to 80 km off earth. Once Timmy passes the 80km mark, he has no way of determining the pressure around him. The science boys find the derivative of his equation and plug in 80 km to approximate what the pressure will be once he leaves 80 km. Using calculus, the science boys found the rate at which pressure was decreasing right before Timmy left the 80km mark. They used this to extrapolate what the atmospheric pressure would be just after Timmy left the atmosphere.

First the science boys got an equation from NASA to find the atmospheric pressure with respect to height below 80km above the Earth.

$$ P = 1035(\frac{1}{2})^{\frac{h}{5.8}} $$

Then the boys noticed that this is a compound function. They'll find the derivative of the oustide function first which is an exponential function. They used the exponent rule: multiply the exponential equation by the natural log of the base.

$$ 1035 \cdot ln(\frac{1}{2}) \frac{1}{2}^{\frac{h}{5.8}} $$

After addressing the exponent, we must address the inside function. We multiply our new function by the derivative of the inside function.

$$ 1035 \cdot ln(\frac{1}{2}) \frac{1}{2}^{\frac{h}{5.8}} \cdot \frac{1}{5.8} $$

Our final function is $ \frac{dP}{dh} = 178.448 \cdot ln(\frac{1}{2}) \frac{1}{2}^{\frac{h}{5.8}}$

Now, we'll plug in 80km (the highest value at which our function will be accurate) so we can approximate the pressure right above 80km. Our functions takes input in centimeters so we must multiply 80 by 100,000.

$$ \frac{dP}{dh}|_{h=80} = -.008713616 \frac{g}{cm^3} $$

Now with this we can approximate the pressure at 80.002 km
